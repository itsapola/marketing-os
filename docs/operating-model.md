# Operating Model

## Overview

Marketing OS organizes marketing work as a connected operating system rather than a collection of isolated tasks.

The model moves from business objectives to execution through a series of structured stages:

```text
Business Objectives
        ↓
Market Understanding
        ↓
Strategic Choices
        ↓
Planning
        ↓
Production
        ↓
Review
        ↓
Distribution
        ↓
Measurement
        ↓
Learning
        ↓
System Improvement
```

Each stage has a distinct purpose.

AI and automation support the work, but they do not own the outcome. Humans remain accountable for strategic choices, quality, interpretation, and final approval.

---

## Operating Model at a Glance

| Stage                | Primary Goal                               | AI Assists With                                              | Humans Own                                                |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------- |
| Business Objectives  | Define what marketing must accomplish      | Synthesis, scenario exploration, briefing                    | Priorities, tradeoffs, accountability                     |
| Market Understanding | Build an accurate view of the market       | Research, clustering, summarization, comparison              | Interpretation, source judgment, implications             |
| Strategic Choices    | Decide where and how to compete            | Option generation, counterarguments, modeling                | Positioning, audience selection, strategic decisions      |
| Planning             | Translate strategy into coordinated action | Drafting plans, timelines, dependencies, risk identification | Scope, sequencing, resource allocation, commitment        |
| Production           | Create assets and deliverables             | Drafting, adaptation, formatting, versioning                 | Creative direction, differentiation, final quality        |
| Review               | Evaluate work before release               | Rubric-based critique, fact checks, consistency checks       | Approval, judgment, risk acceptance                       |
| Distribution         | Deliver work through the right channels    | Scheduling, formatting, tagging, routing                     | Channel strategy, prioritization, relationship management |
| Measurement          | Understand performance and outcomes        | Data aggregation, pattern detection, reporting               | Interpretation, causality, investment decisions           |
| Learning             | Convert results into reusable knowledge    | Summarization, classification, documentation                 | Lessons, changes in belief, future decisions              |
| System Improvement   | Improve the workflows themselves           | Workflow analysis, test generation, regression checks        | Governance, redesign, retirement, ownership               |

# Stage 1: Business Objectives

## Goal

Define the business outcome marketing is expected to support.

Marketing activity should begin with an explicit connection to the organization’s priorities.

Examples include:

* Increase qualified pipeline
* Improve category awareness
* Enter a new market
* Increase product adoption
* Improve customer retention
* Support a product launch
* Strengthen brand preference
* Reduce sales friction
* Reposition the company
* Improve executive visibility

## Inputs

* Company strategy
* Revenue goals
* Product priorities
* Customer objectives
* Leadership direction
* Budget
* Timelines
* Organizational constraints
* Historical performance

## AI Can Assist With

* Summarizing strategic documents
* Identifying inconsistencies
* Mapping objectives to possible marketing outcomes
* Generating scenarios
* Surfacing missing information
* Drafting executive briefs
* Challenging assumptions

## Humans Own

* Choosing priorities
* Making tradeoffs
* Allocating resources
* Resolving conflicting goals
* Defining acceptable risk
* Committing to outcomes

## Outputs

* Marketing objective
* Success criteria
* Strategic constraints
* Decision owner
* Measurement plan
* Key assumptions

## Quality Standard

A strong objective is specific enough to guide decisions and broad enough to avoid prescribing tactics prematurely.

Weak:

> Increase engagement.

Stronger:

> Increase qualified demand among mid-market healthcare organizations by improving category understanding and clarifying the product’s operational value.

# Stage 2: Market Understanding

## Goal

Build a current, evidence-based view of the market, customer, category, and competitive landscape.

This stage reduces the risk of making decisions based on assumptions, outdated information, or internal bias.

## Inputs

* Customer interviews
* Sales conversations
* Product usage data
* Competitive websites
* Analyst reports
* Search behavior
* Reviews
* Industry publications
* Win-loss analysis
* Existing research
* Market data

## AI Can Assist With

* Summarizing large research sets
* Grouping themes
* Comparing competitors
* Extracting claims
* Identifying message patterns
* Mapping customer language
* Detecting contradictions
* Building research tables
* Generating research questions

## Humans Own

* Source selection
* Source credibility
* Interpretation
* Context
* Significance
* Distinguishing signal from noise
* Deciding what the evidence means

## Outputs

* Market landscape
* Customer needs
* Audience segments
* Competitive map
* Category narratives
* Unmet needs
* Risks
* Research gaps

## Quality Standard

Market understanding should distinguish between:

* Evidence
* Interpretation
* Assumption
* Hypothesis

These categories should not be blended together.

# Stage 3: Strategic Choices

## Goal

Turn market understanding into a clear set of choices.

Strategy requires deciding not only what to pursue, but also what not to pursue.

## Inputs

* Business objectives
* Market research
* Audience insights
* Competitive analysis
* Product strengths
* Brand strengths
* Constraints
* Available resources

## AI Can Assist With

* Generating strategic options
* Comparing alternatives
* Simulating competitive responses
* Producing counterarguments
* Identifying internal inconsistencies
* Running pre-mortems
* Stress-testing positioning
* Mapping tradeoffs

## Humans Own

* Target audience selection
* Positioning
* Differentiation
* Brand choices
* Channel prioritization
* Investment decisions
* Risk acceptance
* Strategic commitment

## Outputs

* Target audience
* Positioning
* Value proposition
* Differentiators
* Strategic narrative
* Priority channels
* Strategic exclusions
* Decision rationale

## Quality Standard

A strong strategy should make choices visible.

It should clarify:

* Who matters most
* What the organization wants to be known for
* Why the audience should care
* Why the claim is credible
* What will not be prioritized

A strategy that attempts to appeal to everyone is not a strategy.

## Goal

Translate strategic choices into coordinated, executable work.

Planning connects ambition to capacity.

## Inputs

* Approved strategy
* Campaign objective
* Audience
* Budget
* Resources
* Timeline
* Dependencies
* Channel requirements
* Review process
* Measurement plan

## AI Can Assist With

* Drafting project plans
* Identifying dependencies
* Creating timelines
* Generating task lists
* Flagging risks
* Producing briefing documents
* Mapping stakeholder needs
* Drafting RACI models
* Creating launch checklists

## Humans Own

* Scope
* Prioritization
* Sequencing
* Resource allocation
* Ownership
* Deadline commitments
* Stakeholder alignment
* Escalation decisions

## Outputs

* Marketing brief
* Campaign plan
* Timeline
* RACI
* Channel plan
* Asset list
* Review schedule
* Risk register
* Measurement framework

## Quality Standard

A plan should make execution easier.

It should answer:

* What are we doing?
* Why are we doing it?
* Who owns each part?
* What is required?
* What could block progress?
* How will success be evaluated?

If the team still needs to reinterpret the strategy during production, the plan is incomplete.

## Inputs

* Approved brief
* Positioning

* Brand system
* Channel requirements
* Audience context
* Examples
* Review criteria
* Source material

## AI Can Assist With

* First drafts
* Variations
* Summaries
* Adaptation by channel
* Formatting
* Transcription
* Asset versioning
* Research support
* Headline exploration
* Structural editing
* Metadata generation

## Humans Own

* Creative direction
* Originality
* Taste
* Emotional judgment
* Brand interpretation
* Narrative choices
* Final language
* Final visual decisions
* Approval

## Outputs

* Campaign assets
* Website copy
* Sales materials
* Content
* Email

* Video scripts

Production should be evaluated against the brief, not against the mere existence of a deliverable.

# Stage 6: Review

## Goal

Evaluate work before it reaches the market.

Review should improve quality without creating unnecessary delay or consensus-driven mediocrity.

## Inputs

* Draft deliverables
* Strategic brief
* Brand standards
* Evaluation rubric
* Source material
* Legal or regulatory requirements
* Stakeholder feedback

## AI Can Assist With

* Fact checking
* Consistency checks
* Readability analysis
* Brand voice comparison
* Accessibility checks
* SEO checks
* Rubric-based scoring
* Missing claim identification
* Contradiction detection
* Version comparison

## Humans Own

* Final judgment
* Brand risk
* Ethical review
* Legal escalation
* Strategic alignment
* Stakeholder tradeoffs
* Final approval

## Outputs

* Approved asset
* Revision notes
* Risk flags
* Decision record
* Final version

## Quality Standard

Review should separate:

* Objective errors
* Strategic disagreements
* Brand concerns
* Personal preference

Not every comment deserves equal weight.

A strong review process improves the work without diluting its point of view.

* Campaign sequence
* Platform requirements
* Partner requirements

## AI Can Assist With

* Format adaptation
* Scheduling
* Metadata
* Tagging
* Routing
* Localization drafts
* Campaign sequencing
* Distribution checklists
* Link validation

## Humans Own

* Channel prioritization
* Timing
* Budget allocation
* Relationship management
* Partner coordination
* Community interaction

## Outputs

* Summarizing lessons
* Categorizing feedback
* Comparing campaigns
* Identifying recurring patterns
* Drafting retrospectives
* Updating knowledge bases
* Extracting reusable insights

## Humans Own

* Interpreting the lesson
* Updating beliefs
* Challenging assumptions
* Deciding what should change
* Identifying transferable insights

## Outputs

* Retrospective

A lesson should be specific enough to influence future action.

> Short-form content performed well.

Stronger:

> Short-form educational videos that opened with an operational problem produced higher completion rates than product-led videos, suggesting the audience responds more strongly to problem recognition than feature explanation.

Improve the operating system itself.

Marketing workflows should not remain static while the market, tools, team, and business evolve.

## Inputs

* Workflow performance
* User feedback
* Error logs
* Adoption data
* Quality scores
* Time savings
* New capabilities
* Changed business needs
* Retrospectives

## AI Can Assist With

* Workflow comparison
* Failure pattern analysis
* Regression testing
* Prompt evaluation
* Documentation updates
* Process mapping
* Automation recommendations
* Test case generation

## Humans Own

* Governance
* Workflow redesign
* Tool selection
* Risk decisions
* Ownership changes
* Retirement decisions
* Standards
* Change management

## Outputs

* Updated workflow
* New version
* Retired process
* Improved rubric
* Revised prompt
* Added automation
* Updated documentation

## Quality Standard

Every workflow should be reviewed periodically against four questions:

1. Is it still useful?
2. Is it producing reliable results?
3. Is it being used?
4. Is there a simpler or better way to achieve the outcome?

A system should not be preserved merely because it exists.

# The Human-AI Responsibility Model

Marketing OS separates work into four responsibility levels.

## Level 1: Human-Led

Use when the work requires high judgment, sensitivity, accountability, or originality.

Examples:

* Positioning decisions
* Crisis communication
* Executive messaging
* Ethical decisions
* Brand direction
* Organizational tradeoffs

AI may support the work, but humans lead every stage.

---

## Level 2: AI-Assisted

Use when AI can accelerate analysis or production while human review remains essential.

Examples:

* Competitive analysis
* Campaign planning
* Website audits
* Messaging development
* Executive reporting
* Content drafting

This is the default model for most Marketing OS workflows.

---

## Level 3: Human-Supervised Automation

Use when the process is repeatable and the risks are understood.

Examples:

* Weekly performance summaries
* Metadata generation
* Content formatting
* Research monitoring
* Quality checks
* Dashboard updates

Humans define the rules, monitor performance, and review exceptions.

---

## Level 4: Automated

Use only for low-risk, rules-based, reversible work.

Examples:

* File naming
* Link validation
* Scheduled data pulls
* Formatting
* Routing
* Tagging
* Version archiving

Automation should not silently expand into higher-risk decisions.

# Workflow Design Standard

Every workflow in Marketing OS should document:

## Purpose

What problem does the workflow solve?

## Trigger

When should the workflow begin?

## Owner

Who is accountable for the outcome?

## Inputs

What information is required?

## Steps

What sequence does the workflow follow?

## Decision Points

Where must a choice be made?

## AI Role

What does AI assist with?

## Human Role

What must a person own?

## Outputs

What does the workflow produce?

## Evaluation

How is quality measured?

## Failure Modes

What is likely to go wrong?

## Escalation

When should the workflow stop and require additional review?

## Feedback Loop

How will results improve the workflow?

# Decision Rights

A modern operating model requires clear decision rights.

| Decision             | Recommended Owner                                 |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Business priority    | Executive leadership                              |
| Marketing objective  | Marketing leadership                              |
| Audience priority    | Marketing leadership with sales and product input |
| Positioning          | Marketing leadership                              |
| Campaign strategy    | Campaign owner                                    |
| Creative direction   | Brand or creative owner                           |
| Final asset approval | Named approver                                    |
| Legal escalation     | Legal or compliance owner                         |
| Channel investment   | Marketing leadership                              |
| Performance action   | Metric owner                                      |
| Workflow maintenance | Workflow owner                                    |

AI should never be treated as the owner of a decision.

# Core Feedback Loop

Marketing OS is designed to create compounding improvement.

```text
Objective
   ↓
Decision
   ↓
Execution
   ↓
Result
   ↓
Interpretation
   ↓
Learning
   ↓
System Update
   ↓
Better Future Decision
```

The operating model is successful when each cycle improves the organization’s ability to understand, decide, execute, and learn.

---

# Operating Standard

A Marketing OS workflow is ready to use when:

* The business problem is clear
* The owner is identified
* The required context is available
* Human and AI responsibilities are defined
* The output standard is documented
* Known risks are visible
* Review points are established
* Success can be measured
* Learning can be captured
* The workflow can be improved or retired

The purpose of the operating model is not to add process.

It is to remove ambiguity, preserve judgment, and create leverage.
